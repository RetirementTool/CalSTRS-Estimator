Important Notes & Resources ▶ expand

The option factor is an actuarial calculation. CalSTRS estimates how long they expect to pay — to you, then to your beneficiary. An older member retiring later → higher factor (shorter payout). A younger beneficiary → lower factor (longer payout).

Option choice matters: 100% passes your full benefit (most expensive) · 75% passes three-quarters · 50% passes half (least expensive)

💡 Multiple beneficiaries: CalSTRS also offers a Compound Option — splitting your benefit among two or more beneficiaries, each with their own percentage. This calculator covers a single beneficiary only.

📋 Preretirement election: You may elect an option before retiring. If you do, you get the higher of your election-date or retirement-date factor. If you die before retiring, your beneficiary receives a lifetime benefit calculated as of your date of death.

🏦 Defined Benefit Supplement account not modeled here — it has separate distribution rules. Contact CalSTRS for details.

Insights

▶ expand

Sweet spot

The 75% option is often the financial sweet spot

The 100% option gives your beneficiary the most but costs you the most monthly. The 50% option is cheapest but may leave your survivor short. The 75% option typically balances the tradeoff best — meaningful survivor income at a moderate reduction to your check.

Risk profile

The downside risk is asymmetric — in your favor

If your beneficiary predeceases you, your benefit automatically rises back to the full Member-Only amount for the rest of your life. The reduction only applies while both of you are living. This makes the election much less of a one-way bet than most people assume.

Inflation

The survivor's payment doesn't keep up with inflation

Your beneficiary's monthly amount is fixed at your retirement — it receives no COLA. A $4,000/mo survivor payment today will have meaningfully less purchasing power 20 years from now. If your beneficiary is much younger than you, factor this into which option you choose.

Preretirement election

Electing early locks in a floor, not a ceiling

If you make a preretirement election, CalSTRS uses the higher of your election-date or retirement-date factor. So if your benefit grows before you retire, you keep the better factor. Electing early is risk-free from a factor standpoint. See CalSTRS preretirement election rules ↗.

Age gap

A younger beneficiary costs more — but not linearly

The option factor drops as your beneficiary gets younger because CalSTRS expects to pay longer. But the penalty accelerates — a 10-year gap costs more than twice as much as a 5-year gap. Try the age slider above to see how your beneficiary's age shifts the numbers.

Household vs individual

Household math favors electing — individual math never does

Viewed alone, staying Member-Only always wins for you — you keep more every month. But when you add your beneficiary's lifetime collections, electing an option almost always produces a higher combined total. The question is whether you're optimizing for your income or your household's long-term security.

Factor timing

The factor is calculated at retirement, not at election

If you elect preretirement, the factor is recalculated at actual retirement using both ages at that time (with the higher-of rule). You can't lock in a younger age's factor by electing early. Plan around your expected retirement-date ages.

Planning observations only — not financial advice. Verify all decisions with CalSTRS or a licensed advisor before making any irrevocable election.
